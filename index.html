PROJECT GOALS

The goal of this project is to analyze adult arrest data across a network of New York State counties to identify relationships between arrest types, geography, time, and several other factors. Specifically, we aim to explore how felony and misdemeanor arrest rates have changed over the years, whether certain counties show disproportionately high rates of drug or violent felonies, and what factors may contribute to these disparities. By examining the breakdown and patterns related to arrest types (both felonies and misdemeanors), we hope to gain a clearer picture of how criminal justice trends vary across New York state. Ultimately, this analysis could provide useful insight into broader social and economic questions surrounding crime.

Model Plans

Based on the EDA above, we plan to investigate two complementary models in the rest of the notebook.

Model 1: Hypothesis test on the HPI / arrest-rate relationship. We will test whether there is a statistically significant relationship between county-level HPI and arrest rates after controlling for time and county. The primary independent variables are HPI and annual percent change in HPI. We will fit a linear regression with county and year fixed effects (a within-county estimator) and report the coefficient on HPI along with its t-statistic and p-value. Figures 4 and 5 motivate this directly: they show suggestive co-movement at the aggregate level, but they also make it obvious that controls are required before any conclusion can be drawn. We are using felony share instead of simply the full arrest count as it is not as directly driven by county population size, making it a cleaner signal of how the nature of criminal activity shifts as housing costs change.

Model 2: Predicting county-year felony arrest rates. We will train a linear regression model to predict the felony share of arrests, defined as Total Felony Arrests divided by Total Arrests, for each county-year observation in the data. We will use HPI, annual percent change in HPI, county and year as predictors. We will fit the model on a 70/30 train/test split and evaluate using R^2 and RMSE on the testing set results. The motivation for this model directly relates to the first model, where model 1 asks whether there is a statistically significant relationship between housing prices and arrest levels, model 2 asks how well housing market signals can actually predict arrest behavior.

Together these two models address the headline questions raised by the EDA: (1) is there a real, within-county statistical relationship between housing prices and arrests, and (2) can housing-market features actually predict the kind of crime that gets prosecuted in a given place and year?

When getting into our predictions for these models, we hypothesize that there will be a moderate negative correlation between felony share and housing prices. High-HPI areas tend to over-police minor infractions like noise complaints or low-level drug possession while their typical residents tend to have access to better funded social services, more economic opportunity and lower unemployment, all signs pointing to higher misdemeanor rates.

Model 1: Hypothesis Test on Main Relationship

Before building a full predictive model, we first test whether a statistically significant linear relationship exists between HPI and felony share at the county-year level. To do this, we use the Pearson correlation coefficient, which measures both the strength and direction of the linear relationship between two continuous variables, producing an r value and a p value. We run this test for both HPI and Annual Change in HPI against felony share.

#Model 1 - NY State Total
from scipy.stats import pearsonr

df_model1 = df_merged[['HPI', 'Annual Change (%)', 'Total Felony', 'Total']]
#Create felony share variable to find percentage of total arrests labeled as felonies
df_model1['Felony Share'] = df_model1['Total Felony'] / df_model1['Total']

#Pearson correlation between HPI and Felony Share
r_hpi, p_hpi = pearsonr(df_model1['HPI'], df_model1['Felony Share'])

#Pearson correlation between HPI Annual Change and Felony Share
r_change, p_change = pearsonr(df_model1['Annual Change (%)'], df_model1['Felony Share'])

#Correlation Interpretation
alpha = 0.05
for label, r, p in [('HPI', r_hpi, p_hpi), ('Annual Change (%)', r_change, p_change)]:
  significant = 'statistically significant' if p < alpha else 'not statistically significant'
  direction = 'positive' if r > 0 else 'negative'
  print(f"\n{label}: {direction} correlation (r={r:.4f}), {significant} at a=0.05 (p={p:.4f})")

Model 2: Predicting Felony Arrest Rates

Building on the findings from our first model, we now construct a linear regression model to predict felony share based on HPI and year. Linear regression is appropriate here because felony share is a continuous variable and our hypothesis test established a meaningful linear relationship between HPI and felony share. We exclude Annual Change (%) as a feature because as we found from our hypothesis testing, outside of NYC there is essentially no correlation between the variable and felony share. We have also excluded County as a variable since HPI is already split by county and therefore captures the geographic variations between counties. Year is included as a continuous predictor to control for the long-run time trend visible in the EDA. We will evaluate the model on a 70/30 train-test split using R^2 and RMSE.

#Model 2
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error, r2_score

#Build target variable and model features
df_model2 = df_merged[['HPI', 'Year', 'Total', 'Total Felony']].dropna()
#Create felony share variable to find percentage of total arrests labeled as felonies
df_model2['Felony Share'] = df_model2['Total Felony'] / df_model2['Total']
X = df_model2[['HPI', 'Year']]

Concluding Thoughts

This project set out to investigate whether housing market conditions, as measured by the Hous Price Index, have a meaningful relationship with the composition of adult arrests across New York State counties from 1975 to 2024. The answer, in short, is yes, but with important nuances. Our hypothesis test established a statistically significant positive relationship between HPI and Felony Share both statewide and outside of NYC, contradicting our initial theory that higher-cost counties would show lower felony proportions. Instead, the data suggests the opposite dynamic: as housing costs rise, the share of arrests labeled as felonies tends to rise with them.

The most likely explanation for this pattern is economic displacement, where as housing becomes less afordable, lower-income residents face greater financial stress, and the offenses that come from these stresses tend to skew felony rather than misdemeanor. The predictive model reinforced this finding, with R2 improving dramatically from when NYC was removed, revealing that New York City operates under a distinct set of dynamics that the broader statewide model cannot capture. Perhaps most importantly, the fact that short-term changes in housing prices showed no significant relationship with felony share, while the overall level of HPI did, suggests that it is the system of chronic, structural housing unaffordability rather than sudden market shocks that tracks with shifts in criminal activity. This ultimately points toward long-term affordable housing policy as a potential lever for influencing arrest patterns, rather than reactive responses to market volatility.
