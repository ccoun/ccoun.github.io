PROJECT GOALS

The goal of this project is to analyze adult arrest data across a network of New York State counties to identify relationships between arrest types, geography, time, and several other factors. Specifically, we aim to explore how felony and misdemeanor arrest rates have changed over the years, whether certain counties show disproportionately high rates of drug or violent felonies, and what factors may contribute to these disparities. By examining the breakdown and patterns related to arrest types (both felonies and misdemeanors), we hope to gain a clearer picture of how criminal justice trends vary across New York state. Ultimately, this analysis could provide useful insight into broader social and economic questions surrounding crime.

An initial exploration of the dataset reveals several noteworthy findings. Across all counties and years, the dataset captures a total of 22,337,937 adult arrests in New York State, with an average of around 6,551 arrests per county per year. New York County stands out as having the highest cumulative arrest count at 4,127,331, which is unsurprising given its dense urban population and high law enforcement activity relative to other counties. Looking at trends over time, 1998 had the highest statewide arrest total, recording 542,981 felonies and misedemeanors. This could suggest that the late 1990s represented a peak period of arrest activity in New York, potentially reflecting policing policies of that era. Finally, examining the overall breakdown of offense severity, misdemeanors make up the majority of arrests at 65.6%, while felonies make up the remaining 34.4%, indicating that most adult arrests in New York State are for lower-level offenses. These early statistics provide a strong foundation for deeper analysis into how these patterns vary across counties and shift over time.

Model Plans¶

Based on the EDA above, we plan to investigate two complementary models in the rest of the notebook.

Model 1 - Hypothesis test on the HPI / arrest-rate relationship. We will test whether there is a statistically significant relationship between county-level HPI and arrest rates after controlling for time and county. The dependent variable is total arrests per county-year (and, in follow-up specifications, Total Felony, Violent Felony, and Drug Felony arrests). The primary independent variable is HPI, with annual percent change in HPI included as an additional regressor. We will fit a linear regression with county and year fixed effects (a within-county estimator) and report the coefficient on HPI along with its t-statistic and p-value. The null hypothesis is that, conditional on county and year, HPI has no association with arrest rates; rejecting it would suggest that local housing-market conditions move with local crime in a way that is not just a shared time trend or a fixed level difference between counties. Figures 4 and 5 motivate this directly: they show suggestive co-movement at the aggregate level, but they also make it obvious that controls are required before any conclusion can be drawn.

Model 2 - Predicting whether a county-year has a high felony share. We will define a binary dependent variable equal to 1 if a county-year falls in the top quartile of the felony / total arrests ratio, and 0 otherwise. The independent variables will be HPI, the annual percent change in HPI, year, and a county-level urbanicity flag (built from the NYC boroughs identified in Figure 3). We will train both a logistic regression (for interpretability of coefficients) and a random forest classifier (to capture non-linear interactions) on a 70 / 30 train-test split, and compare them on accuracy and ROC-AUC. The motivation here is operational: if local housing-market signals can predict shifts in the composition of arrests rather than just the level, that is a more actionable finding for both academic and policy audiences.

Together these two models address the headline questions raised by the EDA: (1) is there a real, within-county statistical relationship between housing prices and arrests, and (2) can housing-market features actually predict the kind of crime that gets prosecuted in a given place and year?
